Exercise 18.2.

If many variables are used, some of them may be highly correlated. For example, parents’ level of education and parents’ annual income are likely heavily correlated on a large sample of people. This would result in having a very low smallest singular value of AA and a very high κ(A)κ(A) leading to high condition number κA→xκA→x. This means that a small change in AA can cause a big change in the resulting xx. But the point of this problem is to capture trends. So a small change in the input data such as somebody having 1 year of graduate education vs. 2 years with the same annual income or 2 point difference in IQ should not substantially affect the importance the solution assigns to different factors.

(a) This solution is based on [2].

Let us simplify the problem by assuming that at every point on the water surface we have ripples angled in all direction. Let αα be the maximum angle at which the ripples curve.

The incoming light ray falls on a surface at some angle ββ to the surface normal and reflects along a line that forms the same angle with the normal but on the other side of it in the plane made by the incoming ray and the normal. See Figure 1. below :


Figure 1.

This means that a light ray from the house that falls in the middle of the lake on a piece of water with no ripples will reflect so it falls in the observers eyes, since both the light source and the observer are at the same height. However, if the reflecting surface is at some angle, then the distance between the light source and the reflecting surface needs to be adjusted for the light to reach the observer. This is depicted in Figure 2. below:


Figure 2.

We see from the Figure 2. that if the reflecting surface is angled with the sharper angle turned towards the light source then the reflection point needs to be closer to the light source than the observer if it is to reach the observer. The situation is reversed when the more oblique angle of the reflecting surface is turned to the light source. Also, as visible from the Figure 2. above, tilting the reflecting surface by αα towards the observer and tilting it by αα away from the observer results in two symmetrical cases.

Here we will calculate the ratio of width and length of the vertical streak as perceived by the observer and not the ratio of width and height as they are at the water surface at the points where the light we see is reflected.

We are going to follow the assumption that the perceived dimension of the object is proportional to the angle the endpoints of the object subtend at the eye (See here and here). The perceived dimension of the object is n⋅γn⋅γ where γγ is the subtended angle and nn is the distance from nodal points to the retina of the eye: something like the diameter of the eyeball.

What limits the length of the vertical streak is the maximum angle αα at which the water ripples (see Figure 2.). We need to calculate the angle γγ from Figure 2. because that is the angle that is subtended at the eye of the observer for the length of the vertical streak of light: α1=π2−β1−αα2=π2+α−β1α2−α1=2αα1+γ+π−α2=πγ=α2−α1γ=2α(5)(6)(7)α1=π2−β1−αα2=π2+α−β1(5)α2−α1=2αα1+γ+π−α2=π(6)γ=α2−α1(7)γ=2α

Using (5)(5) and (6)(6) we arrived at the angle γ=2αγ=2α as the angle subtending the length of the light streak at the observer’s eye.

Width of the streak varies at different points. We will use the point in the middle of the lake for our calculations. The actual width on the lake in the middle is the biggest width the streak attains on the lake. However, according to [2] that is not the point of the maximum perceived length. Nevertheless, that is the point they use to caculate the width and so will I, because the calculations are easy. Besides, the width of the streak varies anyways.

(b) This problem is related to the way ∥δy∥2‖δy‖2 behaves when we tilt the range of AA by a fixed δαδα in different planes/around different axes. When the range is tilted in the plane 0−b−y0−b−y, ∥δy∥2‖δy‖2 attains its maximum. If the range is tilted in the plane passing through bb and yy that is perpendicular to segment 0−y0−y, ∥δy∥2‖δy‖2 attains its minimum value that is smaller than its maximum by a factor of sinθsin⁡θ. Similarly, in this problem we get different values for the width and the length of the vertical streak with the maximum length achieved by tilting the reflecting surface in the plane SOT2SOT2 i.e. around rotational axes perpendicular to that plane. If we tilt the reflecting plane around other axes at other points in the lake, the dimension of the streak shortens. We can see that particularly when we observe the width of the light streak in the middle of the lake as we did in part (a) of the problem. The approximate ratio of the width and the length has a similar formulation to the ratio of maximum and minimum ∥δy∥2‖δy‖2 for a fixed δαδα: sinωsin⁡ω with ωω in the case of this problem being the angle ∠OMT2∠OMT2, i.e. the angle between the line connecting the observer and the middle point of the lake on the plane perpendicular to the lake containing the observer and the source and the horizontal line on the lake passing through that plane.

Exercise 18.4.

The condition number of yy becomes 00 with respect to perturbations of AmxnAmxn when m=nm=n because an assumption was made that AA has full rank. Since m=nm=n that means that AA is non-singular. We know that for small enough perturbations EE of a non-singular AA, B=A+EB=A+E is non-singular (for the proof see Theorem 3 in this write-up). Condition number is defined in the limit for small perturbations, so we may assume that the perturbed BB is non-singular and full rank, much like AA. Since both AA and be BB are square matrices of full rank and same dimensions, their ranges are the same. We know that yy is a projection of bb into the range of AA. With perturbations it becomes a projection of bb into the range of BB. Since the two ranges are the same, the projection remains the same across different perturbations. Therefore, the condition number for yy with respect to perturbations in AA becomes 00 when m=nm=n.
